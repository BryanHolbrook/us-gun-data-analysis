Introduction¶

Dataset Description¶

The following dataset is from the FBI's National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS). The NICS is used to determine whether or not a potential gunbuyer is eligible to legally purchase firearms or explosives. Firearms sellers call into the NICS system to confirm the customer does not have a criminal record or is ineligible to make a purchase. The columnal data contains the number of firearm checks by month, state, and type as recorded by gun shop owners and pawn shops selling guns in a given month. Data exists only if a call was made and does not include private sales.

Below is a list of data categories listed in the csv.

'month', 'state', 'permit', 'permit_recheck', 'handgun', 'long_gun', 'other', 'multiple', 'admin', 'prepawn_handgun', 'prepawn_long_gun', 'prepawn_other', 'redemption_handgun', 'redemption_long_gun', 'redemption_other', 'returned_handgun', 'returned_long_gun', 'returned_other', 'rentals_handgun', 'rentals_long_gun', 'private_sale_handgun', 'private_sale_long_gun', 'private_sale_other', 'return_to_seller_handgun', 'return_to_seller_long_gun', 'return_to_seller_other', 'totals'

The NICS data can also be supplemented with U.S. state level census data csv for further insights. Most variables just have one data point per state (2016), but a few have data for more than one year.

The github repository's README containing the FBI NICS Data lists important details needed to understand the data including possible methods for analysis. The details are as follows:

...The FBI’s background check numbers come with caveats: ... many checks are for concealed carry permits, not actual gun sales. ...the FBI’s numbers don’t include private gun sales, many of which do not require a background check. Despite those vagaries, the FBI’s NICS numbers are widely accepted as the best proxy for total gun sales in a given time period.

...Note: Sales estimates are calculated from handgun, long gun and multiple-gun background checks. Permit checks and other categories of background checks are excluded. In California, multiple-gun checks were excluded because data was inconsistent. Because state laws differ, sales levels between states cannot be directly compared.

...To convert background checks into estimated sales, we (New York Times) relied on a method suggested in the Small Arms Survey by Jurgen Brauer, a professor at Georgia Regents University. Each long gun and handgun check was counted as 1.1 sales. Each multiple-gun check was counted as two sales. Permit checks and other types of checks were omitted. The multiplier is an estimate based on Mr. Brauer's interviews with gun shop owners.

Question(s) for Analysis¶

Question 1:¶

What (5) US states/territories have had the highest gun sales in the last (30) days?

Question 2 :¶

What is the overall trend of gun purchases in California in the last (36) months?

Data Anlysis Method¶

Because of the caveats stated above in the data description section, I have chosen theses questions and will be using the Small Arms Survey method designed by Jurgen Brauer, a professor at Georgia Regents University. A method previousy used by the New York Times in their 2015 research piece on US Gun sales estimates:

  • Each long gun and handgun check was counted as 1.1 sales.
  • Each multiple-gun check was counted as two sales.
  • Permit checks and other types of checks were omitted.
  • In California, multiple-gun checks will be excluded because data is inconsistent.

Note: The multiplier is an estimate based on Mr. Brauer's interviews with gun shop owners

Findings include that the top (5) U.S. States/Territories with the highest estimated Gun Sales in the last (30) days in 2017 based on the available NCIS data were (in descending order):

  1. Texas with 91334.5 Gun Sales
  2. California with 67920.6 Gun Sales
  3. Florida with 66304.8 Gun Sales
  4. Pennsylvania with 58351.7 Gun Sales
  5. Ohio with 41743.3 Gun Sales

Additional research can be done by also comparing our findings with the U.S. States/Territories with top (5) highest gun sales per capita amount. Additionally more research can be done on U.S. gun sales from 2017 onward to today tracking the growth or decline over time. Additional information, such as consistent month-to-month data could be useful as many states have missing or incomplete data for many months each year which in its present state would lead to incomplete findings when performing analysis.

Some of the largest limitations in my exploration had to do with incomplete data in regards to missing months, lack of consistecy in Californias 'multiple' guns category, and private gun sales not being included in the data set. Due to the missing data from month to month, lack of private gun sales and general underreporting, has directly impacted the completeness of findings regarding the answer to my questions.

Correlation does not mean causation and although theses are the top (5) states with the highest gun sales in the last (30) days it does not mean these states are the top (5) per capita or per citizen nor does it imply that gun sales are projected to go up indefinitely in to the future as gun control legislation and consumer sentiments may change with time.

Question 2: What is the overall trend of gun purchases in California in the last (36) months?¶

Findings appear to show that the (5) U.S. States/Territories Gun sales appeared to be on an upward trend from 2014 through to the beginning of 2017 with massive spikes in gun sales and almost doubling in the month of December of each year. This trend then reverses its path and falls back down at the beginning of 2017 to early 2014 sales levels, continuing the trend downward still.

Additional research can be done in the years onward from 2017 onward to today tracking the growth or decline over time to confirm this possible downward trend in gun sales following 2017. Additional information necessary specifcally for my question is the missing data month-to-month. Half of the data for 2017, 2016, 2015 did not exist so the question ended up spanning over four years to make up for the loss of gun sales data.

Limitations with my exploration came in the form of missing months data in each year as well as the incomplete data for 'multiple' gun purchases category for the state of California. Due to its inconsistency in collection in the state, 'multiple' had to be disregarded all together for California gun sales totals so there is a clear situation of potential underreporting of gun sales totals.

Correlation does not mean causation and although it does appear that gun sales are trending downward in 2017 that does not guarantee lower sales into the future. As we saw the with the number of gun sales peaking at the end of each year by almost a factor of (2) this trend does seem highly proabable in the coming months and onward.
